Distributed denial-of-service attacks targeting Internet of Things devices represent one of the fastest-growing categories of cybersecurity threat. Gibson Muiruri Mwangi is researching detection and mitigation strategies specifically suited to IoT environments — where devices are resource-constrained, heterogeneous, and deployed at massive scale. The project approaches the problem through traffic analysis, anomaly detection, and machine learning classification, with a focus on methods lightweight enough to run close to the network edge.

## Problem Statement

Traditional DDoS detection systems are designed for servers and enterprise infrastructure with abundant compute resources. IoT networks present a fundamentally different challenge:

- **Resource constraints** — IoT devices (sensors, cameras, embedded controllers) have limited CPU, memory, and battery capacity, making heavyweight detection impractical on-device.
- **Scale and heterogeneity** — A single IoT deployment may span thousands of devices running different firmware, protocols, and operating systems.
- **Attack surface** — Compromised IoT devices are frequently recruited into botnets (such as Mirai) that generate volumetric DDoS traffic directed at external targets.
- **Detection latency** — Slow detection windows allow attacks to saturate bandwidth before mitigation can engage.

## Research Approach

The project investigates a layered detection strategy that combines multiple analytical techniques:

1. **Traffic pattern analysis** — Establishing baseline network behaviour per device class and flagging statistical deviations in packet rate, destination distribution, and protocol usage.
2. **Anomaly detection** — Applying unsupervised learning methods to identify outlier traffic events that deviate from learned normal behaviour without requiring labelled attack data.
3. **ML-based classification** — Training supervised classifiers on labelled IoT traffic datasets to distinguish attack categories (UDP flood, SYN flood, HTTP flood) from legitimate traffic.
